⚖️ Rights & Responsibilities of Engineers (Ch 6)

Pagano tests EXACT definitions. Know the difference between "unalienable rights" and "professional rights." Know what "Right of Conscientious Refusal" means. This connects to the Bill of Rights and NSPE obligations.
Unalienable Rights DEFINITION
Universal and nontransferable rights. They are pre-political — not created by persons or society but rather inherent standards, similar to human rights.

Connected to the United States Bill of Rights — the first 10 amendments to the Constitution. Largely composed by James Madison. Moved to the National Archives on December 13, 1952 with armed guards.

Professional Rights of Engineers
  • Right to privacy
  • Right to participate in activities of choice away from work
  • Right to reasonably object to company policies without fear of retribution
  • Right to "due process"
  • Most fundamental: The Right of Professional Conscience (also called Right of Conscientious Refusal)
Professional Responsibilities (NSPE Obligations)

Along with rights come responsibilities — these are the NSPE obligations:

  • 1. Obligations to the Public — safety paramount, honest reports, report violations, no bribes, don't associate with dishonest organizations
  • 2. Obligations to Employers & Clients — only work within competence, respect confidentiality, disclose conflicts, no kickbacks, no double dipping
  • 3. Obligations to Other Licensed Engineers — don't lie on resume, don't offer bribes, don't harm reputation, DO point out errors when public safety is at risk
Defense Industry Ethics Dilemma

One of the largest employers of engineers worldwide is the defense industry. Weapons are designed to kill human beings.

While some engineers find it morally unacceptable, many reasonable professionals find it acceptable to help defend a nation against hostile aggression. This is a legitimate ethical debate, not a clear-cut answer.

Confidentiality & Proprietary Information
Confidentiality: The state of keeping or being kept secret or private.
Proprietary Information: Material relating to a company's products, business, activities — including financial data, trade secrets, product designs, marketing plans, schematics, client lists, computer programs, etc.
Competitive Bidding

Historically, engineering society codes prohibited competitive bidding (similar to law and medicine).

Original reasons: seemed undignified, could make price more important than safety.

In 1978, the US Supreme Court ruled societies could no longer prohibit competitive bidding. Several states retained laws. The Paradyne case is a direct example of problems with competitive bidding.

🔀 Conflict of Interest (Ch 6)

Know the EXACT definition and all THREE types. Also know the NSPE case study (Engineer A and the village road) and the BER Case No. 69-13. He may ask you to distinguish between them.
Definition & Three Types KEY
A situation in which a person is in a position to derive personal benefit from actions or decisions made in their official capacity.

Three types of Conflict of Interest:

  • 1. Actual Conflict of Interest — a real, existing conflict
  • 2. Potential Conflict of Interest — a conflict that could develop
  • 3. Appearance of a Conflict of Interest — looks like a conflict even if one doesn't technically exist

Can an engineer manage Conflict of Interest? YES! But it takes care and communication — full disclosure is essential.

NSPE Case No. 07-1: Engineer A & the Village Road

Facts: Engineer A works for the State DOT as a district engineer and chairs the state licensure board. He owns land near a village served by a dirt road. During highway construction, he approves a change order to divert road materials to build/surface the road to his village — directly benefiting his property.

Question: Was it ethical? NO.

NSPE Sections violated: II.4.a (disclose conflicts), III.1.e (don't promote self-interest at expense of profession), III.5 (don't be influenced by conflicting interests)

BER Case No. 69-13: Engineer B & the Water System

Facts: Engineer B recommends a water and sewer system in the general area of his own land interest.

Key difference from Case 07-1: Engineer B's project was in the "general area" (less direct benefit) vs. Engineer A who specifically diverted resources to his own property. Both are problematic, but A is a more egregious violation.

📢 Whistleblowers (Ch 6)

Know the definition, the 4 conditions for whistleblowing, the categories (Internal/External, Acknowledged/Anonymous), and the Willful Whistleblower case (Jim, Bob, Ken). Also know Ralph Nader's role. Whistleblowers are legally protected from retaliation.
Definition & Origin DEFINITION
Whistleblower: One who reveals something covert or who informs against another. Especially: an employee who brings wrongdoing by an employer or other employees to the attention of government or law enforcement.

Origin of the term: Attached to law enforcement officials in the 19th century due to their use of whistles. Sports referees who blow whistles for fouls were also called whistleblowers.

Key fact: A whistleblower is commonly protected legally from retaliation.

Until the early 1970s, whistleblowing was seen in a very negative light in corporate America. People were ostracized and put their jobs on the line. Ralph Nader's 1971 Conference on Professional Responsibility helped turn the situation around.

Categories of Whistleblowers
  • Internal — reports within the organization
  • External — reports to outside authorities/media
  • (Both are often seen as disloyal)
  • Acknowledged — willing to put their name behind the accusations
  • Anonymous — reports without identifying themselves
4 Conditions for Whistleblowing KEY

Per Harris, Pritchard, and Rabins (2000), only move forward if ALL FOUR are met:

  1. Need — clearly established wrongdoing
  2. Proximity — firsthand knowledge (hearsay is NOT adequate)
  3. Capability — ability to see the case through to completion
  4. Last Resort — all other avenues of action have been exhausted/shut off
How Companies Can PREVENT Whistleblowing
  • Establish a strong corporate ethics culture
  • Management sets tone for ethical behavior
  • Provide clear lines of communication (honest, open, clear, ethical, humble)
  • A willingness for management to admit mistakes
The Case of the Willful Whistleblower (Jim, Bob, Ken)

Setting: Fairway Electric, nuclear division. Bob is newly promoted VP. Ken Deaver is CEO/mentor.

Jim Bower discovers a 15-year-old report showing Fairway knowingly sold the Radon II nuclear reactor design with known structural flaws. The flaw wasn't a safety hazard but caused construction delays and cost overruns passed to customers (and ultimately ratepayers).

The memo from the old nuclear division head said: problems don't present safety hazard, discontinuing sales would be "counterproductive," customers rarely complain about extra costs.

Key discussion questions:

  • Was Jim right to expose the truth? (Yes — ethical duty)
  • Was Bob right in his advice? (Depends on approach)
  • Was Ken right in his strategy? (Questionable — tried to bury it)
  • What could each have done differently?

🌍 Environmental, Computer & Research Ethics (Ch 7)

Know the anthropocentric vs. equal standing debate, the two types of computer ethical issues, and what "conflict of interest" means in research. The Hartford Civic Center case is a computer ethics example.
Environmental Ethics
Anthropocentric: Western ethical tradition that says only humans have moral standing. Plants and animals are important only in their usefulness to humans.

Key debate: Do we belong to nature, or does nature belong to us?

Alternative view: Humans are just one component of the environment and all components have equal standing (similar to rights and duties). Everyone's duty is to maintain a healthy biosphere for its own sake.

Cost-benefit approach: Balance benefits of reducing pollution against costs. Problems: costs are hard to assess, who pays vs who benefits, is morality being considered?

Bottom line: Professional codes tell us to hold public safety/welfare to highest standards, so our work MUST be conducted in the most environmentally safe manner possible. Also: consult experts (biologists, physicians, public health scientists) in areas outside our competence.

Relevant terms: Sustainable Design, Green Engineering — both related to environmental ethics.

Computer Ethics KNOW THIS

Two types of computer ethical issues:

  • Type 1: When a computer is used to commit an unethical act (hacking, stealing, privacy violation, harassment, copyright infringement)
  • Type 2: When a computer is used as a valid engineering tool but used improperly (e.g., a crashed FEA, incorrect assumptions in CAD)

Legitimate computer uses in engineering: documentation, CAD/CAM, FEA, simulation, data storage, operating machinery, AI/autonomous systems

Opened 1975. Featured a "space truss" roof — one of the first major projects designed using computer-aided design software.

The error: Software assumed joints had no eccentricity (incorrect assumption). This led to incorrect bracing design for 30-foot roof members → significant buckling under snow loads.

Collapse: Hours after a hockey game, roof collapsed under 4.5 inches of wet snow. No deaths — building was empty.

Finding: Roof had been failing incrementally from the moment it was installed. Some members were overloaded by 850%.

Key concept: Concentric (ideal) truss vs. Eccentric truss — load offset from joint center creates bending moment.

Research Ethics

Research must be unbiased and results must be reported truthfully.

Keep ALL possible Conflict of Interest out of ALL research — private or public.

Other unethical behavior in research:

  • Ghostwriting — having someone else write without credit
  • Plagiarism — not properly acknowledging contributions of others
HW #5: Code of Ethics for Computer Use (Problem 7.1)

5 canons for computer use by engineers:

  1. Respect Privacy — don't access/share personal data without authorization
  2. Use Computers Only for Lawful Purposes — no pirated software, no illegal content
  3. Take Ownership of Credentials — don't share accounts, protect logins
  4. Use Computing Resources with Care — don't degrade shared systems
  5. Access Only What You're Authorized to Access — report unauthorized access

NSPE Case No. 10-1: PE Uses Obscene Language in Blog Post

Facts: Engineer A reads a public online blog about a local construction project. Disagrees with the author and writes a lengthy response with coarse, abusive, and obscene language. Includes his name and P.E. designation.

The Scandal

In 2014, West Virginia University tested VW diesel cars on highways and found they produced up to 40x the permitted NOx emissions.

VW had installed "defeat devices" — software that detected emissions testing and reduced output to pass, then reverted to high-emissions mode during normal driving.

Background: The Clean Diesel Plan

In 2006, CEO Bernd Pischetsrieder created a 10-year plan to triple US sales using "Clean Diesel" vehicles.

The dilemma: Diesel engines produce little CO2 (good for climate) but emit NOx (causes smog). The solution was AdBlue — when mixed with NOx, it creates nitrogen and water. But AdBlue requires an extra tank (adds weight, maintenance, cost).

Greenwashing: Making something appear more environmentally friendly than it actually is. VW's "Clean Diesel" marketing was greenwashing.

Key difference: European standards focused on greenhouse gases (CO2) — diesel was good. US EPA's Clean Air Act focused on clean air/NOx — diesel was bad. California's CARB had even stricter standards.

VW's Response & Consequences

CEO Martin Winterkorn apologized, blamed "terrible mistakes of a few people," then resigned.

Replaced by Matthias Mueller.

Board member Olaf Lies: "Those who allowed this acted criminally. They must take responsibility."

VW recalled 11 million cars worldwide, pledged €6.7 billion (~$7.3B) for repairs.

Positive outcome: The scandal accelerated VW's (and the industry's) shift toward electric vehicle development.

Connection to Normalized Deviance

The UVa research paper analyzed VW through Diane Vaughan's concept of normalized deviance (first coined analyzing the Challenger explosion).

Normalized deviance: "A long incubation period with early warning signs that were either misinterpreted, ignored, or missed completely" — Diane Vaughan (1996)

VW's corporate culture made the scandal inevitable. The aggressive sales targets, pressure to minimize the AdBlue tradeoff, and competitive pressure created conditions where cheating became normalized.

Key Facts
  • TMI Unit 2 (TMI-2) — one of the most serious accidents in US nuclear history
  • Located on the Susquehanna River — people lived right next to it
  • TMI was a Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR) (not Boiling Water Reactor/BWR)
  • US has ~95 reactors: 64 PWRs and 31 BWRs
  • Core had 121 fuel assemblies
  • Resulted in a partial meltdown of the reactor core
  • Cleanup completed December 1993, cost over $1 billion
What Went Wrong
  • Hydrogen Bubble: Formed inside the reactor vessel after the meltdown. Feared it could explode — risk not immediately communicated
  • Avalanche of Alarms: Operators were paralyzed by too many alarms during the initial crisis
  • Improper Safety Procedures: Contractors (Bechtel) and GPU Nuclear bypassed safety protocols to save time and money
  • Whistleblower Rick Parks: Warned that "haphazard" cleanup methods could release lethal radiation
  • Contaminated Water: Over 2 million gallons of radioactive water processed
  • Radioactive Gas: Krypton-85 vented, raising environmental and health alarms
The China Syndrome & Public Response

The movie "The China Syndrome" came out just weeks before TMI. Starred Jack Lemmon, Jane Fonda, Michael Douglas. Based on real nuclear plant incidents (including a stuck needle at the Dresden plant near Chicago).

President Jimmy Carter made a very public visit. Carter was a B.S. Nuclear Engineer (Naval Academy) and M.S. Nuclear Physics (Union College).

Governor Dick Thornburgh managed state response. Too many officials giving information — no one knew who to believe.

TMI Reopening for Microsoft

Constellation Energy signed a $1.6 billion power purchase agreement with Microsoft.

The Trump administration announced a $1 billion federal loan to restart the plant for Microsoft data centers.

TMI's working reactor was shut down in 2019 — couldn't compete with cheaper energy sources.

The deal would create ~3,400 jobs, add $16 billion to Pennsylvania GDP, and generate $3+ billion in taxes.

🌀 Hurricane Katrina & New Orleans Levees

Know: 1,900+ deaths, $143B damage, 80% of New Orleans flooded, 50+ levee breaches, USACE responsible, 17th Street and London Avenue Canal failures, and Tara Hoke's ASCE article. This is called the "worst engineering catastrophe in US history."

Overview

20 years ago, hurricanes Katrina and Rita struck the Gulf Coast within weeks. Combined: 1,900+ deaths, $143 billion in damage.

Katrina's storm surge extended 10 miles inland. In New Orleans, it overwhelmed levees designed by the US Army Corps of Engineers (USACE).

50+ breaches. 80% of New Orleans flooded. Thousands rescued from rooftops by Coast Guard.

The 17th Street and London Avenue Canal failures were labeled the "worst engineering catastrophe in US history" (ASCE).

Engineering Failures & Ethical Issues

NOT widespread criminal corruption — instead, "macro-ethical" failures:

  • Flawed design assumptions: USACE failed to properly account for soil strength, used outdated hurricane data
  • 17th Street Canal soil: Boring samples spaced across 1.5 miles despite high soil variability. Factor of safety = 1.3 (low end of accepted values, below Corps's own guidelines). Overestimated soil strength by 30%
  • Outdated hurricane model: Designed for 100 mph winds. When National Weather Service updated projections to 151-160 mph, Corps failed to update their design. Katrina had 125 mph winds
  • Incorrect elevations: Built using land-based datums incorrectly believed equal to mean sea level. Plus subsidence of 0.2 inches/year not accounted for. Some structures 2 feet lower than designed
  • No central authority: Responsibility fractured among multiple agencies
  • Political/cost pressures: Government pushed to lower design standards for affordability
ASCE Code of Ethics Application (Tara Hoke Article)

Canon 1: "Engineers shall hold paramount the safety, health and welfare of the public"

Guideline 1(a): "Engineers shall recognize that the lives, safety, health and welfare of the general public are dependent upon engineering judgments, decisions and practices"

Key lesson from ASCE's External Peer Review: "In the face of pressure to save money or make up time, engineers must remain strong and hold true to the requirements of the profession's canon of ethics, never compromising the safety of the public."

Katrina demonstrates the costs of failing to be vigilant in honoring the duty to protect the public.

📝 FE Exam Ethics Coverage

Know the 4 steps to get a PE license and all 3 obligation categories with key rules. This was a class exercise — each group presented one section.
How to Obtain a PE License (4 Steps)
  1. Earn a 4-year degree from an accredited engineering program
  2. Pass the Fundamentals of Engineering (FE) exam
  3. Complete 4 years of progressive experience under a PE
  4. Pass the Principles and Practice of Engineering (PE) exam
FE Ethics: 3 Obligation Categories (Detailed)

1. Obligation to the Public:

  • Public welfare is MOST important
  • Designs MUST be SAFE
  • MUST report when overruled judgment endangers public
  • Be HONEST in reports and testimony
  • Do NOT guess/speculate when offering professional opinions
  • Do NOT take bribes
  • Do NOT associate with dishonest organizations
  • MUST report violations to state boards
  • MUST cooperate with investigating bodies

2. Obligation to Employers & Clients:

  • Only take assignments within qualifications
  • Do NOT practice outside expertise
  • Do NOT approve unsupervised work
  • MAY delegate to other qualified licensed engineers
  • MUST respect confidential information
  • MUST NOT take kickbacks
  • MUST disclose conflicts of interest
  • NO double dipping (income from two sources for same work)
  • NO divided loyalties / inside lobbying

3. Obligation to Other Licensees:

  • Do NOT lie on your resume
  • Do NOT offer bribes
  • Do NOT harm another engineer's reputation
  • DO constructively point out errors when public safety is at risk

Materials Testing Fraud Case (Tara Hoke, July 2025)

Testing company owner falsified inspection reports for precast concrete on a port expansion project. PE sealed the false documents. A whistleblower from the construction company contacted federal authorities.

9 indictments. PE surrendered license, retired. Company shut down permanently.

ASCE violations: 1a (public safety), 1c (truthful opinions), 1d (zero tolerance fraud), 3b (legal compliance)

Key lesson: "Faithful service" to a client does NOT excuse violations of ethical standards.
